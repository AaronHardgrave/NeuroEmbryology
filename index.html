Formation of Neural Plate, Neural Tube, and Neural Crest Cells

The central nervous system (CNS) begins to form during the third week of development as a slipper-shaped plate of thickened ectoderm called the neural plate (Figure 1a). Positioned cranial to the primitive node and extending toward the buccopharyngeal membrane, the neural plateâ€™s lateral margins elevate to create the neural folds (Figure 1b). These folds give rise to neural crest cells (Figure 1c) and gradually converge at the midline, fusing to form the neural tube. Fusion starts in the cervical region and proceeds both cranially and caudally, leaving temporary openings known as the cranial and caudal neuropores.

Closure of the cranial neuropore occurs around the 25th day (Figure 3) while the caudal neuropore closes 3 days later. Failure of neuropore to completely close can lead to spina bifida.

Spina Bifida Types

  • Occulta: Malformed vertebrae
  • Meningocele: Membranes protrude through vertebral defect
  • Meningomyelocele: Neural tissue exposed through spinal opening

Neuropore Closure

Cranial neuropore closes around day 25; caudal neuropore closes around day 28.
